I spent way too many hours this month juggling three different API tabs, three different pricing pages, and three different Slack threads with coworkers arguing about which model to route our agent pipeline through. If you've ever tried to pick a "best" frontier model in 2026, you already know the problem: every vendor ships a benchmark chart where they conveniently win, and every blog post comparing them reads like it was written by whichever company paid for the placement. I wanted something less biased, so I actually burned credits testing all three on real tasks instead of trusting marketing copy. What started as "let me just check pricing real quick" turned into a full weekend of side-by-side testing, refund emails to myself for wasted tokens, and a spreadsheet I'm mildly proud of.

This post is that spreadsheet, turned into words. I'm not going to tell you one model wins across the board, because that's not how it played out. Each of these three has a spot where it's genuinely the best tool for the job, and each has a spot where it embarrassed itself.

## The Contenders, Quickly

Claude Fable 5 is Anthropic's Mythos-class model, currently their most capable generally available release. It runs a 1M token context window, 128K max output, and is priced at $10 per million input tokens and $50 per million output tokens through the Claude API. It also ships with safety classifiers that can quietly reroute cybersecurity, biology, or chemistry-adjacent requests to Claude Opus 4.8 instead of answering directly, something worth knowing before you build an integration around it.

GPT-5.5 is OpenAI's current frontier model, sitting at $5 per million input tokens and $30 per million output tokens, with a context window just over 1M tokens and 128K max output. There's also a GPT-5.5 Pro variant for the truly hard stuff, priced six times higher, aimed at research-grade math and retrieval work.

Gemini 3.1 Pro is Google's flagship reasoning model, priced at $2 per million input and $12 per million output under 200K tokens (it steps up to $4/$18 above that threshold). Depending on which surface you access it through, the context window is quoted anywhere from 1M to 2M tokens, and native multimodal input covers text, images, audio, and video in one pass rather than bolted-on extensions.

## Coding and Agentic Work

This is where I actually spend my time, so it's where I looked hardest.

Fable 5 was built with agentic operation as a first-class citizen, not a feature added later. Anthropic's own numbers put it at the top of FrontierBench, and in my own testing it handled a genuinely nasty multi-file refactor without losing track of what it had already touched two hundred messages earlier. The thing that impressed me most wasn't raw output, it was that at higher reasoning effort it seems to actually check its own work before handing it back. Fewer "looks done" moments that turn out to be half-done.

GPT-5.5 leans hard into agentic coding too, and it shows. It scores 82.7% on Terminal-Bench 2.0 and matches GPT-5.4's per-token latency despite being noticeably smarter, which is not a small engineering feat. If your workflow lives inside Codex or anything that leans on native computer use (clicking around actual interfaces, not just writing code), GPT-5.5 currently has the more mature story there. I had it navigate a staging admin panel and fill out a form based on a screenshot, and it just did it, no scaffolding required on my end.

Gemini 3.1 Pro is the one that surprised me. It's not chasing the same agentic-coding crown, but its SWE-bench numbers and grounded, low-hallucination behavior on long documents made it my pick for anything involving a huge pile of source files I needed summarized or cross-referenced accurately. Where it stumbled was structured visual output, ask it to generate or manipulate an SVG diagram and the results were noticeably weaker than either competitor. I ended up routing those specific tasks elsewhere.

None of these are "the best at coding" in some absolute sense. They're the best at different flavors of coding, and pretending otherwise is how you end up over-optimizing for a benchmark instead of your actual job.

## Tool Use and Ecosystem

Fable 5 is designed to run inside agent harnesses like Claude Code for days at a time, planning across stages and delegating to sub-agents without much hand-holding. That's a genuinely different use case than "answer my question well." If you want a model that disappears into a background job and comes back with a finished PR, this is the strongest option I tested, assuming your task doesn't trip its cybersecurity or biology classifiers, because then you're quietly talking to Opus 4.8 instead and might not even notice unless you're watching for it.

GPT-5.5's advantage is breadth. It's plugged into the widest surface area of tools, computer use, search, code execution, and the Codex ecosystem specifically has been adopted internally at OpenAI in a way that suggests real dogfooding rather than a demo. If your stack already leans on OpenAI's tool suite, switching feels frictionless.

Gemini's edge is native multimodality. It doesn't stitch together separate models for video and audio understanding, it processes them in one pass. That mattered a lot on a project where I needed a model to watch a screen-recording bug report and reason about both the visual glitch and the console audio cue at the same time. Neither of the other two handled that combination as cleanly.

## Cost, Which Is Where Things Get Ugly

Let's just put the numbers next to each other, because pricing pages love to bury this.

- Claude Fable 5: $10 input / $50 output per million tokens
- GPT-5.5: $5 input / $30 output per million tokens (Pro tier jumps to $30/$180)
- Gemini 3.1 Pro: $2 input / $12 output per million tokens under 200K context, stepping to $4/$18 above that

Gemini is meaningfully cheaper across the board, and it's not a rounding error, it's roughly a fifth of Fable 5's cost per output token. If you're running high-volume agentic loops where the model is calling itself repeatedly, that gap compounds fast and can be the difference between a sustainable product and a line item that gets your project killed in a budget review.

That said, cheaper isn't automatically better. I ran the same eval suite across all three and Fable 5's higher price bought fewer retries on genuinely hard tasks, meaning the effective cost per completed task narrowed the gap somewhat. GPT-5.5 sat in the middle on both price and reliability, which is a fine place to be if you don't want to think too hard about it. The honest takeaway: if you're doing simple, high-volume work, Gemini's pricing is hard to argue with. If you're doing complex, low-volume, high-stakes work, the sticker price stops mattering as much as the failure rate.

## Availability Headaches Nobody Warns You About

Here's something I learned the hard way and wish someone had told me before I built a demo around it. Claude Fable 5 briefly had its access suspended in mid-June 2026 to comply with US export control requirements, then restored on July 1 once those controls were lifted. If you're building anything with real customers depending on uptime, that's a risk worth budgeting for, not just a footnote. Gemini 3.1 Pro is still technically labeled a preview release on some endpoints, which comes with its own "this could change under you" caveat. GPT-5.5 has had the most boring, uneventful rollout of the three, which sounds unglamorous until you're the one on call at 2am.

## Where Each One Actually Wins

If I had to boil down a weekend of testing into three sentences, it'd be this. Reach for Fable 5 when the task is long, ambiguous, and you'd rather hand it off and check back tomorrow than babysit every step. Reach for GPT-5.5 when tool use and computer interaction are the actual bottleneck, not just raw reasoning. Reach for Gemini 3.1 Pro when the budget matters, the input is multimodal, or you're processing a mountain of documents that need to stay factually grounded.

None of these are winning some overall championship, and I'm suspicious of anyone who tells you one is. They're solving overlapping but different problems, priced according to different bets about what you'll use them for.

---

I ended up splitting our pipeline across two of the three, using Gemini for high-volume document triage where cost per call actually adds up, and Fable 5 for the smaller number of genuinely hard, long-running coding tasks where getting it right the first time is worth the extra spend. GPT-5.5 is sitting in a staging branch waiting for a computer-use feature we haven't shipped yet, and I suspect it'll earn a permanent spot once we do.

If you're making this decision for your own team, don't trust a single benchmark chart, including this post. Run your actual workload through all three for a week. The gap between "wins on paper" and "wins on your weird internal task" is bigger than any of these companies want you to notice.
