I went spelunking through six months of server logs last week for a completely unrelated reason, mostly trying to figure out why a caching rule wasn't behaving, and I stumbled onto something that stopped me mid-scroll. My sitemap.xml, the file I dutifully regenerate on every deploy like it's still 2012, was getting hit maybe a dozen times a month. My RSS feed, which I actually put real effort into keeping clean, had even less traffic than that. Meanwhile my llms.txt file, which I added almost as an afterthought a couple months back, was getting requested daily by half a dozen different AI crawlers. I sat there for a solid minute just staring at the numbers, because these were files I'd been told my entire career were non-negotiable infrastructure, and apparently almost nothing was reading them anymore except the tools I'd never designed them for.

That log dive turned into a genuine rabbit hole, and I want to walk through what I found, because I don't think sitemaps and RSS are "dead" in the dramatic sense people like to declare things dead on social media. They're just quietly being replaced as the primary discovery mechanism, and understanding why says a lot about where the web is actually headed.

## What Sitemaps and RSS Were Actually For

Worth rewinding for a second, because it's easy to forget these formats solved real, specific problems for their era. Sitemap.xml exists because search engine crawlers in the early 2000s were bad at discovering deep pages on a site through links alone, especially on large sites with content buried behind search bars or pagination. You handed Googlebot a flat list of every URL you wanted indexed, plus some metadata about how often it changed, and that made crawling dramatically more efficient.

RSS solved a different but related problem: how do you know when a site you care about publishes something new, without manually checking it every day? You subscribed to a feed, your reader polled it periodically, and new content showed up automatically. For a solid decade, this was genuinely how a huge chunk of the internet consumed blogs and news. Google Reader was basically the front page of the internet for a certain kind of person.

Both formats assumed the same underlying model: a crawler or a reader periodically pulls a structured list of URLs, then a human or a search algorithm decides what to do with them. That assumption held up fine for a very long time.

## Where It Started Breaking Down

The first crack wasn't AI, honestly, it was just the slow death of the RSS reader itself. Google Reader shutting down in 2013 is the moment most people point to, and it really did gut casual RSS usage almost overnight. Feed consumption never fully recovered because social platforms and algorithmic feeds ate the "show me what's new" job that RSS used to do. Why subscribe to fifteen blogs individually when a timeline just surfaces things for you, badly ranked but effortlessly delivered.

Sitemaps held on longer because they kept doing their actual job well, feeding search engine crawlers. But even there, modern crawlers got dramatically better at discovering content through link graphs, internal linking, and JS rendering, which quietly reduced how load-bearing your sitemap actually was for a lot of sites. It didn't stop mattering entirely, but it stopped being the make-or-break file it once was for anyone with reasonably well-structured internal links.

Then AI crawlers showed up and changed the equation again, in a way neither format was built to handle at all.

## Why AI Crawlers Don't Care About Either

Here's the part that actually surprised me once I looked closer. AI systems like the ones behind ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Claude aren't trying to index every URL on your site the way Googlebot historically did. They're trying to answer a specific question well, using the smallest, cleanest slice of content that gets the job done. A sitemap gives them a flat list of a thousand URLs with zero context about which ten actually matter. That's close to useless for a system operating under a token budget and trying to decide what's worth ingesting.

RSS has an even weirder mismatch. It's built around chronology, what's newest, in a strict reverse order feed. But AI systems mostly don't care what's newest, they care what's most relevant and most authoritative for a specific question. A five year old, well written explainer is often more useful to a model than something published yesterday. RSS structurally can't express that kind of relevance at all, it only knows how to say "this came after that."

So you end up with two formats that were built to answer questions nobody's really asking anymore. "What's every URL on this site" and "what's the newest thing published" just aren't the questions driving discovery in an AI-mediated web. The actual question is closer to "what on this site would actually help answer this specific query," and neither sitemap.xml nor RSS was ever designed to express that.

## What's Quietly Replacing Them

This is where llms.txt and similar structured formats come in, and it's worth being honest about what they're actually doing differently. Instead of a flat exhaustive list, llms.txt is a curated, human-written summary pointing at the pages that actually matter, with plain language context about why. It's the difference between handing someone a phone book and handing them a short note that says "here's what we do, here are the five pages that explain it best."

That curation piece is the whole shift. Sitemaps and RSS were both built on the assumption that completeness was the goal, list everything, deliver everything chronologically, let the consumer sort it out. AI-era discovery formats assume the opposite, that relevance and context are the goal, and completeness is actually kind of a liability if it drowns out what matters.

I'm not saying rip out your sitemap tomorrow. Google and Bing still use it, and there's no real cost to keeping it around for traditional search. RSS still has a small, loyal audience of people who genuinely prefer feed readers, and that's a fine niche to keep serving. But if you're wondering why your traffic patterns feel weird, why these old reliable files feel quieter than they used to, it's not a glitch. It's a real, measurable shift in how content actually gets discovered now, and the tools built for the old model just weren't designed to compete in the new one.

---

I didn't delete either file, for what it's worth. I just stopped treating them as the primary discovery mechanism and started treating them as legacy support, the digital equivalent of keeping a fax machine around because one client still insists on it. All my actual energy went into the llms.txt file instead, writing real summaries, picking real priority pages, and being honest about what the site is actually for.

The traffic difference wasn't instant or dramatic, this isn't a growth hacking story. But the crawler logs told the real story before the analytics did. Something out there was reading the new file every single day, and almost nothing was reading the old ones anymore. That's usually a pretty reliable signal about where to spend your next twenty minutes of effort.

RSS and sitemaps aren't going anywhere overnight, standards on the web have a way of lingering long after their peak relevance. But if you're building for how people and machines actually discover content in 2026, worth asking honestly which files in your project are still doing real work, and which ones you're just regenerating out of habit.
