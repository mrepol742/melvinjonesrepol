Choosing between Forza Horizon and The Crew Motorfest sounds simple until you've actually spent a decent amount of time with both. They both let you drive incredible cars across huge maps, but after a few hours it becomes obvious that they're trying to deliver completely different experiences. One game constantly reminds you how good it feels to cruise with amazing music in the background. The other just wants to throw you into another race, another vehicle, or another crazy activity without wasting your time. Neither approach is wrong, but they definitely appeal to different kinds of players.

I kept switching back and forth between them, expecting one to completely replace the other. Instead, I found myself opening each game for different reasons. That's what made this comparison interesting enough to write about.

## The Music Makes a Bigger Difference Than You Think

One thing Forza Horizon absolutely nails is its soundtrack.

It isn't just background noise while you're driving around. The radio stations, the opening sequences, and the music during festivals create moments that stick in your memory. Even years later, many players can still remember the songs from the intro or the feeling of driving into the Horizon Festival for the first time.

If you're someone who enjoys driving around without worrying about objectives, Forza simply creates a better atmosphere. Sometimes I would drive for thirty minutes without starting a race because the combination of scenery and music was enough.

Motorfest approaches things differently.

Music exists, but it never feels like the centerpiece. Instead, Ubisoft clearly focused on making the vehicles themselves the stars. Engine sounds are aggressive, races are louder, and everything pushes you toward action instead of relaxing drives.

That said, the engine sounds still don't quite match Forza's overall audio quality. They're enjoyable, but Forza simply sounds more polished.

## Motorfest Isn't Just About Cars

This is probably the biggest difference between both games.

Forza Horizon is primarily about cars. Sure, there are a few special events, but everything revolves around driving.

Motorfest expands that idea dramatically.

Instead of limiting you to cars, it lets you jump into:

- Motorcycles
- Airplanes
- Speed boats
- Off-road vehicles
- Hypercars
- Drift cars
- Rally machines

The transitions between vehicle types happen naturally, making the game feel more like a motorsports playground than a traditional racing game.

Sometimes you're flying over the island. Minutes later you're racing across water before switching back into a supercar. That variety keeps the experience fresh much longer.

## The Open World Is Massive

Motorfest's island feels surprisingly large.

There's plenty of room to explore, and because aircraft are available, the world opens up in ways that simply aren't possible in Forza. Flying across mountains or performing aerial stunts changes how you experience the map.

Forza's map is beautiful and carefully designed, but it doesn't offer that same vertical freedom.

The downside is that Motorfest's world can feel a little empty at times.

The map is huge, yet there are moments where you're simply traveling across long stretches without much happening. The size is impressive, but bigger doesn't always mean busier.

Forza feels more alive during exploration even though the playable area is smaller.

## Events and Progression

When it comes to events, Forza still has the advantage.

The races feel polished, progression is satisfying, and every activity feels like it belongs in the overall festival theme.

Motorfest certainly has plenty to do, but some playlists feel more like collections of challenges than memorable racing events.

If you're the kind of player who enjoys completing races one after another while unlocking cars naturally, Forza provides the smoother experience.

## Loading Into the Action

This was something I noticed almost immediately.

Motorfest launches incredibly fast.

You start the game, load in, and you're practically racing within moments. The transitions from cinematic scenes into gameplay are smooth enough that they almost disappear. Ubisoft deserves credit here because it makes the game feel modern and responsive.

Forza isn't slow, but Motorfest simply feels quicker to get you back behind the wheel.

It's a small detail until you realize how often you restart or jump back into the game.

## Performance Isn't Perfect

Forza Horizon still feels more optimized overall.

Frame rates are generally more stable, gameplay feels smoother, and the experience is consistently polished across long sessions.

Motorfest, on the other hand, occasionally struggles with FPS depending on what's happening on screen. It's not enough to ruin the game, but it's noticeable if you're used to Forza's smoother performance.

Hopefully future updates continue improving optimization because the foundation is already solid.

## Car Customization vs Car Detail

This is where things get interesting.

Forza wins when it comes to customization.

There are more tuning options, better visual modifications, and more freedom to create a car that feels uniquely yours. Whether you're building a drift machine or a clean street car, there's simply more room to experiment.

Motorfest takes a different victory.

The actual cars often look incredible.

The detailing on many vehicles is excellent, especially when viewed up close. Paint reflections, interiors, lighting, and exterior finishes feel carefully crafted. Sometimes I'd spend more time rotating a car in the garage than actually driving it.

So while Forza lets you personalize your car more, Motorfest often makes the cars themselves look more premium.

## Driving Feel

Both games are arcade racers, but they don't feel identical.

Forza strikes a nice balance between realism and accessibility. Cars have weight, different classes behave differently, and driving feels predictable enough that you can push harder with confidence.

Motorfest leans further into fast-paced action. The handling is responsive, races are energetic, and switching between completely different vehicle types keeps things exciting.

Neither is objectively better.

It really depends on whether you're looking for a driving simulator with arcade elements or an action-heavy motorsports experience.

## Which One Would I Recommend?

If music matters to you, Forza Horizon is still difficult to beat. The soundtrack, atmosphere, polished events, stable performance, and customization all come together to create an experience that's easy to remember long after you've stopped playing.

If you're more interested in nonstop racing with different types of vehicles, quick loading, cinematic transitions, and a massive playground where you can drive, fly, or sail almost whenever you want, The Crew Motorfest offers something Forza simply doesn't.

---

At the end of the day, I don't think one game completely replaces the other. Forza feels like a carefully organized festival where every detail has been polished over time. Motorfest feels like a giant playground that constantly encourages you to try something different. Depending on my mood, I still find reasons to launch both, and that's probably the biggest compliment I can give either game.
