I've run all three of these on real hardware, not just inside a VM I fired up once
and never touched again. At some point I had Arch on my main workstation, Ubuntu on
a secondary machine, and Linux Mint on an old laptop I handed to a family member who
responds to "just restart it" with genuine panic. Each one taught me something
different, and not always the lesson I expected. The Linux community loves to argue
about distros like it's a personality trait, but after living with all three, I think
the real answer is boring: it depends entirely on what you're trying to do and how
much friction you're willing to accept.

This isn't a benchmarking post. It's more like field notes from someone who's broken
their system at 11pm and had to figure out why.

## The Philosophy Gap Is Real

Before getting into specifics, it's worth understanding that these three distros aren't
just different flavors of the same thing. They represent genuinely different ideas about
what Linux should be.

Arch is built around the idea that you, the user, should understand your system. Not in
a hazing-ritual way, though it can feel that way sometimes. In a "you make the decisions
and you own the consequences" way. There's no installer that makes choices for you. You
partition your own disk, you choose your own bootloader, you install only what you need.
The payoff is a system where you know exactly what's running and why. The cost is that
getting there takes a weekend.

Ubuntu's philosophy is closer to "just works." Canonical makes a lot of decisions for
you, ships a ton of software out of the box, and has historically focused on making Linux
accessible to people who don't want to read documentation for fun. It's also deeply
commercial in ways that have annoyed parts of the community over the years (Snap packages
being the most recent flashpoint), but it's stable, widely supported, and when you Google
a Linux problem, there's probably an Ubuntu-flavored answer on the first page.

Mint sits somewhere interesting. It's built on Ubuntu, so it gets all that broad
compatibility and package availability, but the Mint team actively pushes back on some of
Ubuntu's decisions. No forced Snaps by default, a more traditional desktop experience, and
a genuine focus on not changing things just to change them. For a lot of people, Mint ends
up feeling like what Ubuntu used to be before Canonical started having bigger ambitions.

## What Arch Actually Gets Right

The reputation Arch has is a bit unfair at this point. The installer is genuinely complex
if you've never touched it, but it's not magic. You follow the wiki, you learn what each
step does, and by the end you've got a system that's exactly as minimal or as loaded as
you made it. I've installed Arch probably eight or nine times across different machines
and the process is meditative now.

What nobody talks about enough is the AUR. The Arch User Repository is legitimately one
of the best things in the Linux ecosystem. If a piece of software exists and runs on
Linux, there's probably an AUR package for it. Maintained by community members, built
from source, and installed with a helper like `yay` or `paru` in one command. Proprietary
tools, obscure utilities, bleeding-edge packages that won't hit Ubuntu repos for another
six months. It's spoiling in the best way.

Rolling releases are the other thing. On Arch you're always running current software. No
waiting for the next major Ubuntu cycle to get a newer kernel or updated GNOME. Updates
come continuously, which is great until it isn't, because sometimes a rolling update does
something unfortunate and you're debugging a broken display server on a Monday morning.
The saving grace is that the Arch community is fast, the wiki is exceptional, and most
breakage gets documented and resolved quickly. You will eventually need to look something
up, and the Arch wiki is so thorough that it helps you even when you're not on Arch.

The honest downside of Arch is time investment. Not just installation time, but ongoing
time. When something breaks, and something will eventually break, you need to care enough
to fix it yourself. If you treat Arch as a set-and-forget system, it will eventually
surprise you.

## Where Ubuntu Earns Its Place

Ubuntu takes criticism from Linux enthusiasts in a way that sometimes feels
disproportionate. Yes, Snap packages have their problems. Yes, some Ubuntu decisions feel
driven more by Canonical's business interests than by pure technical merit. But Ubuntu is
also the reason a huge number of people use Linux at all, and that's not nothing.

Driver support on Ubuntu is genuinely good. NVIDIA drivers, Wi-Fi chipsets, printers,
Bluetooth headphones that behave strangely on other distros. Ubuntu tends to handle all
of this with less friction than most alternatives. If you're setting up a machine for
development work and you want to spend your time actually developing rather than chasing
a Wi-Fi driver, Ubuntu is still one of the safest picks.

The LTS releases are a specific strength. Ubuntu 24.04 LTS gets five years of security
updates. For servers, for work machines, for systems where stability matters more than
having the newest version of anything, LTS releases are a solid foundation. Most of the
cloud and DevOps world runs on Ubuntu precisely because it's predictable. If you've ever
deployed to a VPS or looked at a production Docker base image, you've almost certainly
been in Ubuntu territory.

The Snap situation is real though. Some packages are Snap-only on Ubuntu now, and Snaps
are slow to start, stored weirdly, and feel bolted on compared to native packages. It's
not a dealbreaker but it's a genuine annoyance that Mint specifically addressed by
blocking Snap installation by default, which is one of the reasons Mint users tend to be
pretty happy about that particular decision.

## Linux Mint Is Underrated and It's Not Even Close

I gave my old laptop running Mint to a family member with basically zero Linux experience.
That was two years ago. I have received zero support calls about it. That tells you
almost everything.

Mint defaults to Cinnamon desktop, which is a traditional, sensible desktop environment.
Panel at the bottom, application menu in the corner, taskbar, system tray. If someone is
coming from Windows, there's barely a learning curve on the desktop itself. Files are
where you expect them, the software manager is clean, and the update manager is
conservative in a good way. It prioritizes security updates first and kernel updates can
be managed manually if you want more control.

The team behind Mint is small but focused. They ship less than Ubuntu, they change less,
and they communicate clearly when something is experimental. There's something refreshing
about a project that isn't trying to be everything. The Mint forums are also genuinely
helpful and not nearly as hostile as some corners of the Linux community can get toward
beginners.

Where Mint struggles is on very new hardware. Since it follows Ubuntu LTS releases, the
kernel it ships with can be older, and sometimes brand new hardware needs a newer kernel
to work properly. There are ways around this (the HWE stack, manually installing a newer
kernel), but it adds steps that undercut some of Mint's "it just works" appeal if you're
on a machine that came out in the last year.

## The Package Manager Question

This is where the Debian/Ubuntu side and the Arch side live in genuinely different worlds.

`apt` is stable, well understood, and has been around forever. The package selection in
the Ubuntu/Mint repos is large but not exhaustive, and packages are often a version or
two behind upstream. For most software this doesn't matter. For newer libraries or tools
under active development, it sometimes does.

`pacman` on Arch is fast and clean. Combined with an AUR helper, it's hard to beat for
sheer availability. The tradeoff is that you're sometimes installing things that are more
cutting edge than you strictly need, and the occasional package conflict or update that
breaks something is the price of admission.

Neither is objectively better. But if you've ever needed a specific version of a tool for
a project and spent an hour trying to wrangle `apt` into giving it to you, the AUR starts
to look very attractive.

## Weird Issues I Actually Ran Into

On Arch, I once had a GNOME update change how fractional scaling worked, which made my
4K monitor look wrong overnight. Took me about forty minutes of forum reading to fix.
Annoying? Yes. But the fix was documented, the thread was recent, and I understood what
had changed afterward. That's the Arch experience in miniature.

On Ubuntu, I fought with the Firefox Snap for longer than I'd like to admit. It's slow to
open, it doesn't integrate with system themes the same way, and trying to replace it with
the non-Snap version requires a bit of terminal work that shouldn't be necessary for a
default browser. I ended up just installing a different browser and moving on, which is
probably what Canonical expects people to do, which is fine but slightly irritating.

On Mint, the only real issue I hit was audio on a slightly older Realtek chip that needed
a config file tweak. Five minutes of research, one file edit, rebooted, done. That was
genuinely representative of the Mint experience: occasionally something small needs
fixing, but it's never dramatic.

## Gaming: A Quick Note

This used to be a non-discussion. Now it matters. Gaming on Linux is actually good in
2026, and which distro you're on affects the experience more than you'd think.

Arch wins here purely on recency. The latest Mesa drivers, the latest Proton versions,
the latest kernel patches for Wayland compatibility. If you're chasing game compatibility
on Proton and want every edge, Arch is where that happens first. You also have the option
to install more niche tools from the AUR without hassle.

Ubuntu is fine for gaming, but occasionally a game that requires a newer version of a
library has you jumping through hoops. The Firefox-Snap situation has an analog in
gaming: some things just aren't as seamless as they should be. That said, Ubuntu with the
latest NVIDIA drivers properly installed is a completely workable gaming setup. Most Steam
games run well.

Mint is good for older titles and anything that doesn't need cutting-edge GPU features.
Cinnamon is lighter than GNOME, so it gives your GPU more headroom. But if you're on a
new RTX card and want the very latest drivers, you're going to be doing a bit more manual
work than on the other two.

## Disk Space and Resource Usage

This rarely gets discussed but it matters on older hardware.

A minimal Arch install is around 800MB to 2GB depending on what you add. A full desktop
installation with GNOME is around 4-6GB but you put every byte there yourself. RAM usage
after boot varies hugely based on your desktop choice, but a lightweight window manager
on Arch can idle at under 200MB.

Ubuntu with GNOME sits around 7-10GB installed and idles at around 700MB-1GB RAM
depending on background services. For most modern machines this is completely fine. It's
not lightweight but it's not particularly heavy either.

Mint with Cinnamon is noticeably lighter than GNOME. Installed footprint is similar to
Ubuntu since they share the same base, but Cinnamon idles at roughly 400-500MB RAM, which
is a real difference if you're on a machine with 4GB or 8GB. This is one of the reasons
Mint became popular as a way to breathe life into older hardware.

## The Documentation Situation

Arch wiki vs Ubuntu community vs Mint forums. This is worth talking about.

The Arch wiki is the best Linux documentation in existence. It's not even close. Detailed,
accurate, kept up to date, and its coverage extends well beyond Arch-specific topics. If
you're debugging something on any distro, there's a reasonable chance the Arch wiki page
about that thing is the most useful resource online.

The Ubuntu community is enormous. Ask Ubuntu on Stack Exchange has answers for basically
every problem you'll encounter, and most of them are detailed. The flip side is that answers
go stale, older threads give outdated commands, and you sometimes have to read five threads
to figure out which solution applies to your current version.

Mint's forums are smaller but the community is genuinely patient with beginners. If you're
not a developer who's comfortable figuring things out alone, the Mint forums are
surprisingly pleasant to use.

## Who Should Use What

If you want to actually understand your Linux system, enjoy reading documentation, want the
newest software, and don't mind occasional breakage that you'll be expected to fix yourself:
Arch is genuinely excellent. It's not for everyone but it rewards investment.

If you're setting up a development machine, a server, or a system where compatibility and
community support matter more than anything else: Ubuntu LTS is still a defensible choice.
It's boring in the right way for professional contexts, and boring is sometimes exactly what
you need.

If you want a Linux desktop that stays out of your way, doesn't change things on you, and
would work fine for someone who just wants to browse the web and open LibreOffice without
thinking about it: Mint is probably the most underappreciated distro in the mainstream
conversation. It's stable, clean, and the Cinnamon desktop is genuinely good.

The worst outcome isn't picking the wrong distro. It's spending so long arguing about
distros online that you never actually build anything. Install something, break it, fix it,
learn something. That's the whole game. And if you hate your first pick, switching is one
afternoon of your time.
